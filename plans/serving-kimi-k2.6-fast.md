# Serving Kimi-K2.6-class models at real-time speed — research findings

**Source:** ultracode workflow `serve-kimi-k2.6-fast` (9 agents: 6 parallel research dimensions → synthesis → 2 adversarial stress passes). Saved 2026-06-09.
**Companions:** `redesign-the-web.md` (why we need fast+smart inference), `confidential-ai-gateway.md` + `dynamic-provider-substrate.md` (the gateway this serves).

> **The question:** can an open-source Kimi-K2.6-class model (~1T total / ~32B active MoE) be served at Cerebras-gpt-oss-class speed (~800–1500 tok/s single-stream), and if not, what is the bottleneck and how far off are we? Numbers are mid-2026; re-verify before quoting.

---

## Verdict

**Real-time feasible: YES** — but only on wafer-scale on-chip-SRAM hardware. Cerebras WSE-3 serves K2.6 at **981 tok/s** (measured). On GPUs it cannot: ~91–123 tok/s naked, ~376–385 tok/s even with speculative decoding (Fireworks, the fastest GPU) — i.e. ~3–10× short for a single stream.

## The bottleneck (what to solve for)

Fast-memory CAPACITY-PER-CHIP forcing multi-chip sharding, which turns batch=1 decode from bandwidth-bound into latency-bound (a per-layer collective/handoff tax on the serial autoregressive critical path).

**Why:** Proof by elimination: (1) bandwidth is oversupplied on the winner -- Cerebras uses 0.075% of its 21 PB/s at 981 t/s; (2) model size isn't binding -- the full 1T model is served and still real-time; (3) kernels aren't the wall -- stacks already hit ~70% of roofline. What remains is irreducible serialization: 61 layers must run in order per token, and because 594GB exceeds any one chip, they're split across devices, so every token eats inter-device handoff (Cerebras inter-wafer pipeline bubbles; GPU TP all-reduce ~25% of decode). The smoking gun: 8xH200 measured 91 t/s = 3.8% of its 2,400 t/s aggregate-BW ceiling; the other 7 GPUs barely cut batch=1 latency. The sparse MoE compounds it -- spec-decode verifying 3 tokens lights up 2.94x the expert bytes, capping EAGLE-3 at 1.6-1.9x.

## How far from optimal

SINGLE-CHIP HONEST CEILING (FP4, MBU=0.7, the 'what one stream could do if TP weren't forced' number): H100 147 t/s, H200 210 t/s, B200/B300 350 t/s. At MBU=1.0: H200 300, B200 500. These are HYPOTHETICAL -- the model cannot fit one GPU. AGGREGATE-BW FANTASY (8-GPU node, INT4, MBU=1): 8xH200 = 2,400 t/s, 8xB200 = 4,000 t/s. ACHIEVED single-stream, no spec: 91 t/s (8xH200) = 3.8% of its 2,400 aggregate ceiling = a 26x shortfall; 111 t/s (8xB200) = 2.8% of 4,000 = a 36x shortfall; 123 t/s (8xRTX6000Pro). The effective realized bandwidth is ~1.46 TB/s = roughly ONE H200 at ~30% MBU. WITH spec-decode/MTP/EAGLE3 (1.6-1.9x on this MoE): Fireworks 376-385 t/s (the fastest GPU), CoreWeave GB300 251 t/s -- still only ~16% of the 2,400 aggregate. CEREBRAS WSE-3: 981 t/s achieved vs a 1.3M t/s on-wafer bandwidth ceiling = it runs at 0.075% of its own theoretical optimum (bound by pipeline depth + fabric latency, NOT bandwidth), and that 0.075% is STILL 2.6-10.8x faster than the best GPU. Per-token latency: Cerebras 1.02 ms, Fireworks 2.66 ms, CoreWeave 3.98 ms, naive 8xH200 ~11 ms. Bottom line ratio vs the 800 t/s real-time floor: naive GPU 6.5x short, tuned-GPU+spec 2.1x short, Cerebras 1.23x OVER.

## Hardware path

On-chip-SRAM / wafer-scale (Cerebras WSE-3) is the only hardware class that clears real-time single-stream for a 1T/32B model, and it already serves K2.6 at 981 t/s. GPUs have capacity but are HBM-bandwidth-bound (500 t/s B200 roofline, 70-385 t/s realized). Groq's 230MB/chip SRAM needs ~2,200+ chips for 1T -- it hosts only the older K2-0905, never K2.6.

## Software path

Software cannot manufacture the missing bandwidth: best stacks already hit ~70% of the HBM roofline, leaving ~1.3-1.5x headroom vs the 2-4x needed. Levers, all largely spent: FP4/NVFP4 or INT4-QAT (halves bytes/token), EAGLE-3/MTP spec-decode (+60-90% = 1.6-1.9x, capped by the MoE union penalty), and vLLM-WideEP/SGLang-PD/TRT-LLM expert-parallel (3-5x DECODE THROUGHPUT but per-stream latency stays flat). SOTA GPU single-stream converges at ~370-385 t/s.

By the architect's OWN 'all-reduce ~25% of decode' figure, collectives explain at most a 1/(1-0.25)=1.33x slowdown; that accounts for ~1.3x of the 26x gap, leaving ~20x unattributed. ## Per-dimension research

### decode-physics

**Real-time verdict:** YES, a K2.6-class model CAN hit real-time single-stream (981 tok/s measured on Cerebras WSE-3, inside the 800-1500 t/s band), but ONLY on memory architectures that hold all active weights on-chip (SRAM at tens of PB/s). On GPUs it CANNOT today: the honest single-chip physics ceiling is just 147-350 t/s/chip (FP4, MBU~0.7), and because 594GB of weights force 8-way TP, single-stream decode becomes latency-bound and lands at 90-123 t/s naked, reaching only 251-385 t/s even with speculative decoding/MTP/EAGLE3 -- i.e., GPUs are ~3-10x short of real-time for ONE stream, while Cerebras clears it. Verdict: real-time K2.6 = on-chip-SRAM hardware (Cerebras-class) is the only path that works in mid-2026; GPUs need either much higher single-chip HBM bandwidth, the model small enough to fit one chip, or larger speculative-decode accept rates (4x+) to close the gap.

**Bottleneck (this lens):** THE single bottleneck for single-stream decode of a K2.6-class MoE is the off-chip memory hierarchy forcing tensor parallelism: 594GB of INT4 weights don't fit on one GPU (80-192GB), so 8-way TP is mandatory, and at batch=1 that turns decode from bandwidth-bound into LATENCY-bound -- a per-layer collective tax (61 layers x TP all-reduce + MoE all-to-all per token over NVLink) plus tiny sharded GEMVs collapse realized bandwidth to ~3-9% of the node aggregate. GPUs hit ~90-123 t/s naked (vs a 2,400-4,000 t/s aggregate-BW fantasy), and only speculative decoding/MTP (2-3x) lifts them to 250-385 t/s. Cerebras sidesteps the bottleneck entirely by keeping all weights in 21-PB/s on-wafer SRAM, eliminating both the HBM wall and the off-chip collective latency.

**Key findings:**
- FORMULA: single-stream decode tok/s = (effective_memory_BW) / (active_params x bytes_per_param). Kimi K2.6 = 1.0T total / 32B active per token (top-8-of-384 routed experts @ 2048 hidden + 1 shared expert + MLA attn over 61 layers, 1 dense). Active-weight read per token: BF16=64GB, FP8=32GB, INT4/FP4=16GB. Halving bytes/param doubles the ceiling.
- HONEST PHYSICS CEILING is the SINGLE-CHIP number (no tensor-parallel tax): at MBU~0.7, INT4/FP4 -> H100 147 t/s (3.35 TB/s), H200 210 t/s (4.8 TB/s), B200/B300 350 t/s (8 TB/s). FP8 halves these (H200 105, B200 175). At MBU=1.0: H200 INT4 300, B200 INT4 500. A Cerebras WSE-3 wafer (44GB SRAM, 21 PB/s) is ~6,500x more bandwidth than one B200, so reading 16GB/token costs ~1us of SRAM time -> NOT bandwidth-bound at all.
- THE TRAP: the naive 'aggregate-BW of the 8-GPU node / active bytes' formula gives 8xH200 INT4 = 2,400 t/s and 8xB200 FP4 = 4,000 t/s -- but real single-stream hits only 91-123 t/s (3.8% of the H200 aggregate, 2.8% of B200). The 594GB of INT4 weights cannot fit on one 80-192GB GPU, so 8-way TP is FORCED, and at batch=1 that converts decode from BANDWIDTH-bound to LATENCY-bound: each 2048-wide expert is sliced into 256-wide GEMVs (memory-latency, not BW, dominated) plus a per-layer collective tax (61 layers x [all-reduce for TP + all-to-all for MoE routing] per token over NVLink). A single stream effectively uses the bandwidth of ~ONE H200 at ~30% MBU; the other 7 GPUs barely cut latency.
- MEASURED REALITY LADDER (mid-2026, Kimi K2.6): batch-1 no-spec = 91 t/s (8xH200 INT4 conc=4, SemiAnalysis), 111 t/s (8xB200 NVFP4 TP4 conc=4), 123 t/s (8xRTX6000Pro conc=1, DCP1+MTP). With speculative decoding/MTP/EAGLE3 (2-3x accept): 251 t/s (CoreWeave GB300 EAGLE3), 376-385 t/s (Fireworks, the fastest GPU). Cerebras WSE-3: 981 t/s -- 6.7x the next-fastest GPU cloud, 23x the median provider. Per-token latency: Cerebras 1.02 ms/tok vs Fireworks 2.66 ms vs H200 10.97 ms.
- QUANTIZATION SHIFT: going BF16->FP8->INT4/FP4 each halves both the weight footprint (2000->1000->500GB, so min GPUs @192GB = 11->6->3) AND the per-token read, doubling the BW ceiling each step. Kimi K2.6 ships INT4 via Quantization-Aware Training (594GB) and runs FP4/NVFP4 on Blackwell, 16-bit-float compute on Cerebras. FP4 is the floor before quality loss; it gets GPUs from the FP8 ceiling (~105-175 t/s/chip) to ~147-350 t/s/chip, still short of real-time without spec-decode.
- WHY CEREBRAS WINS (the physics): it holds all weights in on-wafer SRAM (44GB/wafer x ~14 wafers for 594GB INT4) at 21 PB/s/wafer and a fabric '>200x NVLink' bandwidth, so it pays NEITHER the HBM bandwidth wall NOR the off-chip TP/all-to-all collective latency that caps GPU single-stream. It is limited only by pipeline depth + fabric latency, landing at 981 t/s -- inside the 800-1500 t/s real-time band.

### hardware

**Real-time verdict:** YES — a Kimi-K2.6-class 1T MoE CAN be served at real-time single-stream speed, and it already is: Cerebras WSE-3 delivers ~981 tok/s on the actual K2.6 model (Artificial-Analysis-verified, 6.7x the fastest GPU cloud). That clears the 800-1500 tok/s real-time bar. The decisive lever is hardware class, not model size: you must put the model in on-chip SRAM with on-wafer interconnect. The cost of doing so is steep but bounded — roughly ~20 CS-3 systems and on the order of ~$30M-$60M capex (est.). On HBM GPUs (B200/GB200, MI325X/MI355X, TPU) the verdict is NO for single-stream real-time today: roofline caps a single B200 at ~500 tok/s (FP4) and MoE all-to-all over NVLink drags real systems to ~70-385 tok/s — exactly matching the user's ~238 tok/s Fireworks reality and the ~6x gap. Groq is a NO for this size: 230 MB/chip SRAM forces ~2,200+ chips for a 1T model, and Groq tellingly hosts only the older K2-0905, not K2.6. Bottom line: real-time K2.6 = buy wafer-scale SRAM capacity; it exists, it's verified, it's expensive.

**Bottleneck (this lens):** THE bottleneck is SRAM CAPACITY (memory capacity per chip), NOT bandwidth and NOT compute. Decode is memory-bandwidth-bound in general, but the moment you adopt wafer-scale SRAM you have so much bandwidth (21 PB/s, ~1300x the requirement) that bandwidth stops binding — and the binding constraint becomes 'can the 1T model physically FIT in fast memory.' WSE-3 holds only 44 GB/wafer, so a ~500 GB (4-bit) model must be split across ~20 wafers. That sharding is exactly what re-introduces the SECONDARY limiter that caps you at ~981 instead of ~1.3M tok/s: cross-wafer all-to-all + the serial per-layer (61 layers) latency of autoregressive decode spread over ~20 systems. Net: capacity forces sharding; sharding forces inter-chip communication; inter-chip communication is the residual speed tax. On HBM GPUs the framing flips — they have ample capacity but the bottleneck is raw HBM bandwidth (8 TB/s caps a single B200 at 500 tok/s FP4) plus MoE expert all-to-all over NVLink, which is why GPUs sit at ~70-385 tok/s. So per the hardware dimension: the ONE bottleneck to solve is fast-memory CAPACITY-PER-CHIP — owning it (Cerebras, via ~20 wafers of SRAM + on-wafer fabric) is what unlocks real-time; renting bandwidth on HBM does not.

**Key findings:**
- IT ALREADY EXISTS, AND IT'S CEREBRAS. Cerebras WSE-3 serves the EXACT target model (Kimi K2.6, 1T total / 32B active MoE) at ~981 output tok/s single-stream, independently verified by Artificial Analysis (the highest speed AA has recorded for any frontier model). That is in the 800-1500 tok/s real-time band. So the answer to 'can a K2.6-class model be served at Cerebras-gpt-oss-class speed' is YES, on Cerebras silicon, TODAY. Source: cerebras.ai/blog/cerebras-kimi-k2-Enterprise, venturebeat, artificialanalysis.ai.
- THE CORE TRADEOFF IS BANDWIDTH vs CAPACITY, and WSE-3 wins by buying capacity with MONEY, not by sacrificing speed. Decode is memory-bandwidth-bound: tok/s_ceiling = effective_bandwidth / bytes_streamed_per_token. For K2.6, 32B active params at 4-bit = ~16 GB must stream per token. WSE-3 on-chip bandwidth = 21 PB/s -> 21e15/16e9 = ~1.3 MILLION tok/s bandwidth ceiling. So on the wafer, bandwidth is ~1300x in SURPLUS; speed is NOT the wafer's problem. The wafer's problem is CAPACITY: only 44 GB SRAM per wafer.
- CAPACITY MATH -> ~20 WAFERS, ~$50M. K2.6 at 4-bit weights = ~1T x 0.5 B = ~500 GB. Naive floor: 500/44 = ~11.4 wafers just for weights. Real Cerebras deployment = ~20 CS-3 systems (confirmed): the ~1.8x overhead over the weight floor is KV cache + activation buffers + the MoE constraint that ALL experts of a layer sit on ONE wafer (so expert all-to-all stays at SRAM speed) + speculative-decode draft + kernel working set. At ~$2-3M/CS-3 (~$1.56M/node at 576-node bulk), ~20 systems = ~$31M-$60M capex. THAT is the price of fitting a 1T model in SRAM.
- ON HBM GPUs, BANDWIDTH (not capacity) IS THE WALL. Single B200 = 8 TB/s, 192 GB. Roofline at FP4: 8e12/16e9 = 500 tok/s THEORETICAL per GPU. Real GPU-cloud single-stream on K2.6 = ~70-385 tok/s (Fireworks 69-376, CoreWeave 251, Together-FP4 342; the cited ~238 tok/s 'kimi-k2p6-fast' sits squarely in this band) = only 14-77% of roofline. The deficit vs roofline AND vs Cerebras is the MoE all-to-all expert-routing collective over NVLink (1.8 TB/s/GPU, 130 TB/s aggregate on GB200 NVL72) — orders of magnitude below on-wafer fabric, which Cerebras states is >200x NVL72's NVLink bandwidth. GPUs have the CAPACITY easily (8xB200 = 1.5 TB >> 500 GB) but can't feed a single stream fast enough.
- GROQ CANNOT REACH K2.6 — IT'S A CAPACITY-COST CLIFF. Groq LPU = 230 MB SRAM/chip (190x smaller than WSE-3's 44 GB). A 1T 4-bit model (~500 GB) needs ~500e9/230e6 = ~2,200 LPU chips MINIMUM (sources: ~hundreds for 70B, ~thousands for 400B). Tellingly, Groq hosts Kimi K2-0905 (the OLDER 1T variant) but does NOT host K2.6, and even consolidated/deprecated its K2 offerings — the chip-count economics of a 1T model on 230MB-per-chip SRAM are brutal. Groq wins SRAM bandwidth-per-chip (80 TB/s) but loses the capacity war so badly the cluster cost/complexity is prohibitive for 1T-class.
- WHY WSE-3 HITS ~981 NOT ~1.3M tok/s: once you shard across ~20 wafers, the bottleneck shifts from on-wafer bandwidth to (a) the serial per-layer dependency of autoregressive decode across 61 layers spread over ~20 systems, and (b) inter-wafer activation streaming latency. Cerebras mitigates with on-wafer fabric for intra-layer all-to-all + custom kernels + SPECULATIVE DECODING (multiple tokens/step). So the realized single-stream ceiling is set by cross-wafer pipeline latency, not raw SRAM bandwidth — but it still lands at real-time.

### serving-stack (vLLM / SGLang / TensorRT-LLM software SOTA for large-MoE decode on GPUs)

**Real-time verdict:** NO -- a K2.6-class 1T/32B-active model CANNOT hit real-time (>800 tok/s single-stream) on today's GPU serving stacks, and the wall is hard. The best GPU single-stream result is ~368-385 tok/s (TRT-LLM on 8xB200 with NVFP4 + 3x-MTP speculation; Fireworks on Kimi K2.6), which is already ~2.3x past the FP8 B200 roofline of ~250 tok/s only because speculative decoding amortizes weight loads. The theoretical FP4-on-B200 roofline is ~500 tok/s and best stacks reach ~70% of it (~350) before speculation. So 800 tok/s needs another clean ~2x and 1500 needs ~4x ON A SINGLE STREAM, with the bytes/param lever (already at FP4) and the speculation lever (already at ~3x acceptance) mostly exhausted -- the residual headroom in the software stack is maybe 1.3-1.5x (closing 70%->~95% MBU + better MTP), not the 2-4x required. The existence proof that the MODEL is fine is Cerebras at 981 tok/s on the identical K2.6: that gap is bought with ~2,600x more on-chip bandwidth (21 PB/s SRAM vs 8 TB/s HBM), which no vLLM/SGLang/TRT-LLM optimization can manufacture. Verdict: real-time for this model class is a HARDWARE-BANDWIDTH problem (wafer-scale SRAM, or HBM4/multi-die bandwidth scaling), not a serving-software problem.

**Bottleneck (this lens):** Within the serving-stack dimension, the single bottleneck for single-stream decode is HBM memory bandwidth versus the bytes-per-token of the active expert set, NOT kernel/software quality. The best stacks (TRT-LLM, vLLM WideEP, SGLang) already extract ~70% of the bandwidth roofline; the remaining ~30% of kernel + MoE-routing/all-to-all + launch overhead is real but second-order. Decode tok/s ~= MBU x HBM_BW / (active_params x bytes/param): with a fixed 32B active params, the ONLY large software lever left is shrinking bytes/param (FP8->FP4 halves traffic, ~2x) or faking parallelism in the token dimension (speculative decoding / MTP, another ~2-3x at ~3x acceptance). Both are largely spent already in the 368-385 tok/s SOTA. Expert parallelism, tensor parallelism, PD-disaggregation, continuous batching, and prefix caching raise AGGREGATE throughput and cost-efficiency but leave per-stream latency on the memory-bandwidth wall.

**Key findings:**
- DECODE IS MEMORY-BANDWIDTH-BOUND, and the bytes-per-token is set by ACTIVE params, not total. For a Kimi-K2.6-class 1T/32B-active MoE, the roofline is tok/s = HBM_bandwidth / (active_params x bytes_per_param). FP8 (1 B/param) => 32 GB moved per token. H200 @ 4.8 TB/s => 150 tok/s ceiling; B200 @ ~8 TB/s => 250 tok/s ceiling. FP4/NVFP4 (0.5 B/param) halves traffic to 16 GB/token => H200 ~300, B200 ~500 tok/s ceiling. (cerebras.ai MoE calculator; arxiv 2507.15465 roofline)
- BEST-IN-CLASS SINGLE-STREAM GPU SERVING TODAY lands at ~368-385 tok/s, NOT by beating the roofline but by ADDING SPECULATIVE DECODING. NVIDIA TensorRT-LLM hit 368 tok/s single-user on 8xB200 for DeepSeek-R1 671B using NVFP4 MoE + 3x Multi-Token-Prediction layers at ~3.1x draft-acceptance + CUDA graphs + fused MLA/grouped-GEMM kernels (5.5x over a 67 tok/s naive baseline). Fireworks serves the actual Kimi K2.6 at ~376-385 tok/s (fastest public GPU provider; FP4). These two independent numbers agreeing (~370 tok/s) is the real GPU single-stream SOTA.
- EFFICIENCY (MBU) OF THE BEST STACKS IS ~70% OF ROOFLINE, not 95%+. Measured: Qwen3-30B-A3B on H100 hit 333 of 466 theoretical tok/s = 71%. The arxiv bottleneck paper reports 30-50% MBU for unoptimized single-stream and ~70% as 'close to roofline.' So even a perfect kernel on a B200 at FP4 tops out near 0.7 x 500 = ~350 tok/s per stream WITHOUT speculation; speculation (MTP) is what pushes Fireworks/TRT-LLM past that to ~370-385.
- THE BIG PUBLISHED MoE THROUGHPUT NUMBERS ARE AGGREGATE, NOT SINGLE-STREAM. LMSYS's Kimi-K2 large-scale EP run = 288k decode tok/s but across 12 H200 nodes (96 GPUs) at BATCH 480 => only ~600 tok/s per request best-case, and that config targets cost (~$0.21/M tokens), trading per-user latency for throughput. vLLM WideEP on GB200 reports 10.1k decode tok/s PER GPU but again aggregate over a large batch. In the memory-bound regime, batching RAISES aggregate tok/s but LEAVES PER-STREAM LATENCY FLAT (weight loading still dominates) -- so you cannot batch your way to a fast single stream.
- DISAGGREGATED PREFILL/DECODE + EXPERT PARALLELISM (EP) + FP4 + fused MoE + CUDA graphs are now mature (vLLM WideEP, SGLang PD, TRT-LLM) and deliver 3-5x DECODE THROUGHPUT vs H200 baseline on Blackwell -- but that gain is overwhelmingly THROUGHPUT-per-dollar, not single-stream speed. SGLang+NVFP4 hits 13.4k decode tok/s/GPU; these are batch numbers. None of EP/TP/PD-disagg changes the single-stream divisor: one token still must stream all 32B active params from HBM.
- CEREBRAS BEATS GPUs ON THE SAME MODEL FOR ONE REASON: BANDWIDTH, NOT SOFTWARE. WSE-3 keeps weights in 44 GB of ON-CHIP SRAM at ~21 PB/s = ~2,600x a B200's 8 TB/s HBM. Same 32 GB/token traffic, ~2,600x bigger divisor => 981 tok/s measured on Kimi K2.6 (6.7x the ~146 tok/s typical GPU host, 23x the ~42 tok/s median host). This is the proof the MODEL can run real-time; the serving STACK on GPUs is not the limiter -- HBM bandwidth is.

### spec-decoding

**Real-time verdict:** NO — a K2.6-class MoE cannot hit real-time (>800 tok/s single-stream) on GPUs via spec-decoding at batch=1, and this dimension says it falls ~2x short. The math: best tuned GPU single-stream today is ~376-385 t/s (Fireworks) and 205-251 t/s (CoreWeave), ALREADY including EAGLE-3 + NVFP4 on Blackwell. The MoE expert-union penalty caps batch-1 EAGLE-3 at ~1.6-1.9x and MTP at ~1.8x (single-token lookahead), so even stacking the most optimistic +90% onto a clean ~250 t/s base lands ~380-475 t/s — roughly 2x under the 800 t/s floor and 3x under 1500. The theoretical BW roofline (~2,000 t/s for 32B-active FP4 on an 8-GPU node) does NOT save you: real systems sit at ~19% of it precisely BECAUSE the spec-verify step on a sparse MoE at batch=1 multiplies expert memory traffic ~3x while acceptance stays ~2.5/3. To clear 800 t/s you need ~3.5x over the 123 t/s native base or ~2.1x over the 385 t/s tuned base, and the union penalty makes that 2.1x physically unreachable for draft+verify at batch=1. The existence proof that K2.6-class CAN run at ~1000 t/s is Cerebras (981 t/s) — but it wins by SRAM bandwidth (~21 PB/s on-chip, ~1000x an HBM GPU's per-token budget), i.e. it solves the memory wall directly rather than papering over it with speculation. Bottom line: the 'fast model proposes, K2.6 verifies' path is real and worth shipping (free 1.6-1.9x, take it), but on GPUs it gets you to ~400-475 t/s, not real-time. Real-time for a K2.6-class model is a BANDWIDTH problem (wafer-scale SRAM, or HBM4e + bigger MTP lookahead), and spec-decoding is a multiplier ON that bandwidth, not a substitute for it.

**Bottleneck (this lens):** For the spec-decoding dimension, THE bottleneck is the MoE expert-activation union at batch=1, formalized by N(t)=E*(1-(1-ρ)^t). Speculative decoding's whole premise is 'verify K tokens for ~1 token's worth of weight reads.' That premise holds on DENSE models (the same weights serve every draft token). It BREAKS on a sparse MoE like K2.6: verifying 3 draft tokens activates ~23.5 of 384 experts instead of 8, i.e. ~2.94x the dominant (routed-expert) memory traffic, so you spend ~3x the bytes to accept ~2.5 tokens. That is why EAGLE-3 on K2.6 yields only 1.6-1.9x where it yields 3-4x on LLaMA-70B. The penalty is structural to sparsity (it shrinks only when you batch enough requests to make all experts resident — B~16-32 — which is the opposite of the batch=1, low-latency regime an interactive generative-OS UI needs). Secondary contributors: lower acceptance on agentic-code text (σ~0.58-0.80 on MoE vs ~0.91 dense), draft-model overhead, and spec-verify-decode kernels that were not CUDA-graph-safe on MLA until early-2026 fixes. Net: spec-decoding can recover ~1.8x of the ~6-8x gap to real-time on GPUs and no more, because the algorithm fights the architecture at batch=1.

**Key findings:**
- K2.6 ships its own EAGLE-3 draft (`kimi-k2.6-eagle3`, MLA-matched to the target) with 3 speculative tokens. Real measured uplift on GPUs is +60-90% generation throughput (= 1.6-1.9x), NOT the 3-4x seen on dense models. This is the single most load-bearing number for this dimension.
- K2.6 is a SPARSE MoE (1T total, 32B active, 384 routed experts, 8+1 selected, ρ=8/384=0.021). That is exactly the worst case for batch=1 spec-decoding: each extra draft token you verify lights up DIFFERENT experts, so memory traffic does NOT amortize the way it does on a dense model.
- Expert-union penalty (MoESD, arXiv 2505.19645): distinct routed experts when verifying t tokens is N(t)=E*(1-(1-ρ)^t). For K2.6: N(1)=8.0, N(3)=23.5, N(4)=31.0. So verifying 3 draft tokens at batch=1 reads ~23.5/8 = ~2.94x the routed-expert weight bytes vs one token. You pay ~3x the dominant memory cost to accept (best case) ~3 tokens -> the speedup collapses toward ~1x in the pure-memory limit, which is precisely why measured EAGLE-3 on K2.6 caps at 1.6-1.9x (it only wins because Fireworks/CoreWeave run well below the BW ceiling, so spare bandwidth absorbs part of the union penalty).
- MTP heads (the 'free' single-draft-head path DeepSeek/Kimi ship): DeepSeek-V3's 2nd-token MTP head hits 85-90% acceptance -> 1.8x TPS. This is a 1-token lookahead, so it sidesteps most of the union penalty (N(2)=15.8 experts, ~2x bytes for ~1.8x tokens = roughly break-even-plus). MTP is the most batch=1-friendly accelerator but caps near ~1.8x because it only proposes one token.
- Acceptance on MoE/agentic-code text is materially lower than the dense-model marketing numbers: EAGLE on MoE ~0.80, Mixtral-class σ~0.58 at γ=4, vs Qwen-dense σ~0.91 (MoESD). Lower acceptance + higher per-token verify cost is the double hit. Mean accepted tokens/step for K2.6 EAGLE-3 at draft-3 is ~2.3-2.6, not the ~3.5-4 of LLaMA-70B EAGLE-3.
- Theoretical roofline leaves room but the algorithm can't reach it at batch=1: a 32B-active FP4/NVFP4 model on an 8-GPU node is BW-bounded at ~2,000 tok/s (80% of aggregate HBM3e/HBM4 bandwidth). Best real K2.6 GPU single-stream today = Fireworks ~376-385 t/s, CoreWeave 205-251 t/s (both already use EAGLE-3 + NVFP4 on Blackwell GB200/GB300). So real systems sit at ~19% of the BW roofline; the gap is the MoE union penalty, draft overhead, and CUDA-graph/kernel inefficiency on the spec-verify decode shape.
- Batch>1 vs single-stream is a direct conflict for an interactive UI. Spec-decoding's MoE penalty actually DISAPPEARS at moderate batch (B~16-32) because all 384 experts are already resident, so the extra draft tokens ride spare compute (MoESD peak 2.29x at moderate B). But a generative-OS UI is batch~=1, low-latency -> you are stuck in exactly the regime where the expert-union penalty is maximal AND batching can't rescue you. You cannot have both batch-throughput economics and batch-1 latency on the same request.
- Cerebras serves the SAME K2.6 at 981 tok/s single-stream WITHOUT needing spec-decoding (cryptobriefing/Cerebras): wafer-scale keeps all 32B active weights in ~21 PB/s on-chip SRAM, so its per-token ceiling is ~1000x the per-token BW of an HBM GPU. This is the existence proof that 'K2.6-class at ~1000 tok/s' is real -> but it is won by BANDWIDTH (SRAM), not by the spec-decoding algorithm. Spec-decoding is the GPU's attempt to fake that bandwidth, and on a sparse MoE it only recovers ~1.8x of the needed ~6-8x.

### gap-and-bottleneck

**Real-time verdict:** YES -- a Kimi K2.6-class (1T-param, 32B-active) model ALREADY hits real-time single-stream today, but only on wafer-scale SRAM hardware, not on GPUs. Cerebras serves the full K2.6 at 981 tok/s single-stream (Artificial-Analysis-verified, 6.7x the best GPU cloud), clearing the >800 tok/s real-time bar and delivering sub-6s generative-UI renders. The reason it works is NOT that K2.6 is small (it isn't) or that bandwidth was raised (the single stream uses <0.1% of WSE-3 bandwidth) -- it is that on-wafer SRAM puts the active weights one hop from compute and the fabric removes the tensor-parallel all-reduce tax that throttles GPUs. On GPUs the same model is ~140-385 tok/s single-stream (~2-6x short of real-time) because (i) it doesn't fit in one device so every token eats inter-GPU sync, and (ii) a single stream can't saturate aggregate HBM bandwidth. So: real-time for K2.6-class is solved on Cerebras/Groq-class wafer-or-SRAM silicon, unsolved on commodity GPUs. To push K2.6-class to Cerebras-gpt-oss-class headroom (1500-3000 tok/s single-stream), the move is to attack the ONE remaining bottleneck -- decode's sequential critical path -- via speculative/multi-token-prediction decode (1.8-2.8x, up to 6.5x) on top of a low-hop SRAM substrate; raising raw bandwidth further buys almost nothing for a single stream.

**Bottleneck (this lens):** THE single primary bottleneck is the SEQUENTIAL NATURE OF DECODE (the per-layer critical path / autoregressive dependency chain), NOT memory bandwidth, NOT model size, NOT kernel/routing efficiency. Proof by elimination with numbers: (1) Bandwidth is ~1000x oversupplied on the winning system -- Cerebras burns ~15.7 TB/s of 21,000 TB/s (~0.075%) at 981 tok/s, so the memory wall is already solved by SRAM and is not binding. (2) Model size isn't binding -- the full 1T params are served and still hit real-time; you do NOT need a smaller/distilled model. (3) Kernels/routing aren't the wall -- MoE routing and FP4 kernels are mature (vLLM/SGLang/TRT-LLM, MTP support shipped). What remains is irreducible serialization: 61 layers must execute in order per token, and because a 1T model exceeds any single chip's fast memory, those layers are split across wafers/GPUs, so every token pays inter-device handoff latency (Cerebras: inter-wafer pipeline bubbles; GPU: TP all-reduce ~25% of decode). At 981 tok/s the serial path is ~1.02 ms/token (~16.7 us/layer). To go faster you must SHORTEN OR PARALLELIZE THE DEPENDENCY CHAIN: (a) fit more layers in fast memory per device to cut hops (Cerebras' '5-6x more SRAM per core' -> full tensor-parallel, no pipeline bubbles), and/or (b) break the autoregressive serialization with speculative / multi-token-prediction decode -- DeepSeek-style MTP + spec-decode delivers 1.8x-2.8x (up to 6.5x) more accepted tokens per serial step, directly attacking this exact bottleneck. The one lever that most unlocks faster real-time is speculative/parallel decode layered on a low-hop (wafer-scale SRAM) substrate.

That clears the 800 tok/s real-time bar. The premise that K2.6-class can't hit Cerebras-class speed is outdated -- it does.
- The 'gap' is NOT a memory-bandwidth gap. Decode bytes/token for K2.6 = 32B active x 0.5 B (Cerebras runs native 4-bit weights, computes in FP16) = 16 GB/token. At 981 tok/s that is only ~15.7 TB/s of effective bandwidth consumed -- versus the WSE-3's 21,000 TB/s (21 PB/s) of on-wafer SRAM bandwidth PER wafer. Single-stream bandwidth utilization is ~0.075%. Bandwidth is ~1000x oversupplied for one stream; it is decisively NOT the binding constraint on the best system.
- THE bottleneck on the best real system is the SEQUENTIAL DEPENDENCY of autoregressive decode, made worse by inter-wafer pipeline bubbles. A 1T model in 4-bit (~500 GB) vastly exceeds one wafer's 44 GB SRAM, so Cerebras chains multiple wafers and splits the 61 layers across them (pipeline parallelism). With TP=8, all-reduce is ~25% of decode time and a SINGLE stream cannot saturate aggregate HBM bandwidth. 8xH200 has a ~1,200 tok/s weights-only ceiling (38 TB/s aggregate / 32 GB FP8) yet single-stream lands ~140-385 tok/s (Baseten 140+ on 8xB200 NVFP4; Fireworks ~238-385). **Bottleneck (this lens):** THE bottleneck for the practical path is HBM memory bandwidth on GPUs in the single-stream (batch=1) regime. Decode re-reads all 32B active params per token (~18 GB at FP4), so per-stream tok/s = node_HBM_bandwidth / bytes_per_token, with a ~2.8x real-world realization penalty. That caps GPU single-stream at ~340 tok/s (8xH200/B200) and ~540 even with EAGLE-3 spec decode -- structurally short of the 800-1500 real-time band, and unfixable by renting more or bigger GPUs (more GPUs add batch throughput, not single-stream speed). Cerebras sidesteps it by holding weights in 44 GB on-wafer SRAM (>200x NVLink bandwidth), which is precisely why it hits 981 tok/s. So the practical bottleneck is really MEMORY TECHNOLOGY (HBM vs on-chip SRAM), and access to the only vendor that has solved it -- Cerebras is currently enterprise-trial-gated for K2.6, so the secondary bottleneck is commercial availability, not engineering.

- Why GPUs are stuck (the floor): batch=1 decode is purely HBM-bandwidth-bound.
